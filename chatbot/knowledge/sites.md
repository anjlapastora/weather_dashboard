# Monitoring Sites

Helios tracks three sites chosen for their contrasting climatic profiles.

---

## Riyadh, Saudi Arabia

- **Key**: `riyadh`
- **Coordinates**: 24.69°N, 46.72°E
- **Timezone**: Asia/Riyadh (UTC+3)
- **Dashboard colour**: Amber (#F59E0B)
- **Climate**: Hot desert (BWh). Dry year-round. Rarely cloudy.
- **Solar profile**: Among the highest irradiance globally — daytime peaks regularly reach 900–1,100 W/m² in summer. Very long clear-sky windows.
- **Wind profile**: Moderate surface winds (10–25 km/h typical), occasional shamal (north wind) events with gusts exceeding 60 km/h.
- **Why it matters**: Ideal case study for utility-scale solar PV due to consistently high GHI and predictable cloud cover.

---

## Wellington, New Zealand

- **Key**: `wellington`
- **Coordinates**: 41.29°S, 174.78°E
- **Timezone**: Pacific/Auckland (UTC+12 / UTC+13 NZDT)
- **Dashboard colour**: Blue (#60A5FA)
- **Climate**: Temperate oceanic (Cfb). Frequently windy, mild temperatures.
- **Solar profile**: Moderate irradiance — lower than Riyadh due to latitude and frequent cloud cover. Seasonal variation is significant (summer peaks ~800 W/m², winter much lower).
- **Wind profile**: One of the world's windiest cities. Average wind speeds 20–40 km/h; gusts of 100+ km/h during Cook Strait storms are not unusual. Strong southerlies are the dominant pattern.
- **Why it matters**: Excellent wind resource — illustrates sites where wind energy is far more viable than solar.

---

## Manila, Philippines

- **Key**: `manila`
- **Coordinates**: 14.60°N, 120.98°E
- **Timezone**: Asia/Manila (UTC+8)
- **Dashboard colour**: Green (#34D399)
- **Climate**: Tropical monsoon (Am). Hot and humid. Distinct wet (Jun–Nov) and dry (Dec–May) seasons.
- **Solar profile**: High irradiance potential but significantly reduced during monsoon season due to heavy cloud cover. Dry season peaks approach 800–900 W/m².
- **Wind profile**: Moderate trade winds. Typhoon season (Jun–Nov) can bring extreme gusts; occasional supertyphoons bring anomalous wind readings.
- **Why it matters**: Demonstrates how tropical weather variability (monsoon cycles, typhoons) affects both solar and wind energy generation and creates anomaly spikes.
